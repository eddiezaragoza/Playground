Background -- What Is This?

Futures in 60 Seconds

A futures contract is an agreement to buy or sell something at a set price in the future. We're trading MNQ -- the Micro E-mini Nasdaq 100 futures contract. It tracks the Nasdaq 100 index (think Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, etc.) but in a bite-sized format designed for individual traders.

The key difference from stocks: futures let you profit from price going up or down. If you think Nasdaq will rise, you "go long" (buy). If you think it'll drop, you "go short" (sell). You don't need to own anything -- you're just betting on direction.

What Is the Opening Range Breakout (ORB)?

The ORB is a morning scalping strategy that's been used by professional traders for decades. Here's the idea in plain English:

  1. Wait for the market to open at 9:30 AM Eastern
  2. Watch the first 5 minutes and record the highest price and the lowest price during that window. This range is called the "opening range."
  3. If price breaks above the high of that range, buy (go long). The logic: buyers are in control, momentum is pushing up.
  4. If price breaks below the low of that range, sell (go short). The logic: sellers are in control, momentum is pushing down.
  5. Set automatic exit orders:
    • A stop-loss on the opposite side of the range (limits your loss if you're wrong)
    • A profit target at 1.5x the risk (so you make more on winners than you lose on losers)
Why does this work? The first few minutes of trading set the tone for the day. Institutional traders, algorithms, and overnight order flow all clash at the open. When price decisively breaks out of that initial range, it often signals the direction for the next few hours. The ORB captures that momentum.

What Is a Backtest?

A backtest takes a trading strategy and runs it against real historical market data to see how it would have performed. It's like a time machine -- "if we had been running this strategy over the past 17 days, here's what would have happened."

The results below are from running the ORB strategy on real MNQ price data from February 3-25, 2026 (17 trading days). Every trade you see actually could have been executed in the market at those prices.

Important caveat Backtests show what would have happened, not what will happen. Past performance doesn't guarantee future results. That said, backtesting is the standard way professional traders validate a strategy before putting real money behind it. The next step is "paper trading" -- running the strategy in real-time with simulated money to confirm the backtest holds up.

Honest Caveats

17 days is a small sample These results are from late Jan through late Feb 2026 -- a strong Nasdaq uptrend. A 67% win rate and 2.9 profit factor would be elite if it held up over 6-12 months. With only 17 days, we don't know how it handles choppy sideways markets or sharp selloffs.
Backtest != live trading Backtests assume perfect fills at the signal price. In real trading, you'll face slippage (price moves between signal and fill), partial fills, and latency. Expect live results to be 10-20% worse than backtest numbers.
Survivorship bias risk All sweep combos were profitable on this dataset. That's a yellow flag -- it likely means the underlying market was strongly directional. A truly robust strategy should show some losing combos too, proving it works despite market noise, not because of a favorable trend.

What Would Build Confidence

  • More data -- accumulate 3-6 months of 1-minute bars by running download-all weekly
  • Paper trading -- run live on simulated money for 20+ trades to compare against backtest expectations
  • Different market conditions -- the real test is performance during a bear market, choppy range, or high-volatility event days
  • Walk-forward analysis -- optimize on one period, test on the next (out-of-sample validation)
