The Vision -- What Are We Building?
15 minutes
+

Before diving into logistics, get aligned on the big picture. This sets the tone for every decision that follows.

  • What kind of events do we want to serve? (weddings, parties, corporate, festivals, all of the above?)
  • What's our vibe? Classy and elegant? Fun and tropical? Rustic charm? Modern and sleek?
  • Who's our ideal customer? (brides? corporate planners? backyard party hosts?)
  • Are we doing this as a side hustle or going full-time eventually?
  • What's our geographic range? How far are we willing to travel for an event?
Key question: If we could only do ONE type of event, what would it be?

The Partnership -- How We Work Together
15 minutes
+

The #1 reason small businesses fail is partnership disagreements. Having this conversation NOW -- when everything is exciting and positive -- is 100x easier than having it during a conflict later.

  • Ownership split: 50/50? Or different based on investment/roles?
  • Who handles what? (Toby: business/marketing? Partner: bartending/events? Or both do everything?)
  • How do we make decisions? Unanimous, or does one person own certain domains?
  • How much is each person investing upfront (money AND time)?
  • What happens if one person wants out someday? (Buy-out terms)
  • How do we split profits? Equal? Based on hours worked? Reinvest first?
Must-do: Write down whatever you agree on. This becomes your operating agreement. Get it on paper even if it's informal.
Research LLC formation in your state ($50-$500 filing fee)
Draft a simple operating agreement (or hire an attorney: $500-$1,500)

The Money -- Startup Budget & Funding
20 minutes
+

Get real about how much this costs and how you'll fund it. The good news: you can start lean and scale up.

Three paths to launch:

  • Lean start (no trailer): $5,000 - $15,000 -- Portable bar setup, basic equipment, business formation. Test the market first.
  • Mid-range (used trailer conversion): $20,000 - $40,000 -- DIY conversion of a horse trailer or vintage trailer. Most popular path.
  • Premium (turnkey trailer): $55,000 - $90,000+ -- New purpose-built bar trailer, professional branding, everything dialed in.

The big costs:

  • The trailer: $3,000 - $55,000 (biggest variable)
  • Conversion/buildout: $8,000 - $20,000 (if DIY or hiring a fabricator)
  • Equipment & tools: $2,000 - $5,000
  • Licensing & legal: $500 - $5,000 (state dependent)
  • Insurance (year 1): $2,500 - $4,000
  • Initial inventory: $1,500 - $4,000
  • Branding & marketing: $500 - $3,000
  • Working capital reserve: $3,000 - $6,000
The question: How much can each of us invest? What's our total budget? Do we need a small business loan?
Each person: write down your max comfortable investment amount
Research SBA microloans and local small business grants

The Rig -- What Kind of Trailer?
15 minutes
+

The trailer IS your brand. It's the first thing clients see and the reason they book you over a standard bartending service.

  • Converted horse trailer -- Most popular right now. Rustic, Instagram-worthy. $3,000-$12,000 for the trailer + $8,000-$20,000 conversion. Look for 16 ft aluminum-frame models.
  • Vintage Airstream/camper -- Iconic retro look. $8,000-$25,000 + conversion. Photographs beautifully but harder to find in good condition.
  • Purpose-built bar trailer -- New from companies like Cedar Trailer Co. $25,000-$55,000 turnkey. Built to code, ready to go, but less character.
  • Portable setup (no trailer) -- Start here to test the market. $1,000-$5,000. Foldable bar, bins, tools. Load into an SUV.
  • Converted van or bus -- Self-propelled, no tow vehicle needed. $15,000-$45,000.
Do either of you have a truck that can tow? Towing capacity matters -- a loaded bar trailer can weigh 8,000-12,000 lbs.
Browse Facebook Marketplace and Craigslist for horse trailers this week
Check your tow vehicle's max tow rating
5
The Legal Stuff -- Licenses & Insurance
15 minutes
+

This is the least exciting part but the most important. Serving alcohol without proper licensing and insurance can result in personal liability lawsuits that wipe you out.

Two ways to operate:

  • Full-service -- You buy and serve the alcohol. Requires a liquor license ($200-$14,000 depending on state). Higher revenue per event.
  • Dry hire -- Client buys the alcohol, you provide the bar, equipment, and bartending. No liquor license needed in most states. Faster to launch, lower risk.

Insurance you MUST have:

  • General liability: $500-$1,200/year
  • Liquor liability: $300-$900/year (even for dry hire -- protects you if a guest you served gets into trouble)
  • Commercial auto/trailer: $800-$2,000/year
  • Total: ~$2,500-$4,000/year

Florida-specific notes (Riverview / Tampa Bay):

  • Florida is mobile-bar friendly! The state offers a Special Catering License through the Florida Division of Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco (FL DABT).
  • A 2COP license (beer & wine) is ~$1,820. A 4COP license (full liquor) is harder to get and much more expensive -- but a catering license is a viable alternative.
  • Hillsborough County (Riverview/Tampa) and Pinellas County (St. Pete) each have their own local business tax receipt requirements on top of state licenses.
  • Florida requires all alcohol servers to complete a Responsible Vendor Training Program (free or low-cost, about 3 hours online).
  • Tampa Bay is a HOT wedding market -- waterfront venues, brewery scene, year-round outdoor events. Perfect for mobile bars.
First question to answer: Do we want to start as dry hire (faster, cheaper) and upgrade to full-service later? Most successful mobile bars start this way.
Call FL DABT at (850) 487-1395 or visit myfloridalicense.com -- ask about Special Catering License
Apply for Hillsborough County Local Business Tax Receipt ($25-$100)
Get insurance quotes from FLIP, Insurance Canopy, or NEXT Insurance
Both partners: complete Florida Responsible Vendor Training (free online)

The Menu -- What Are We Serving?
10 minutes
+

Your signature cocktails are your differentiator. This is where your bartender friend's expertise really shines.

Trending cocktails right now:

  • Espresso Martini (#1 trending cocktail nationally)
  • Spicy Mango Margarita
  • Aperol Spritz
  • Lavender Collins
  • Smoked Old Fashioned
  • Paloma variations

Menu strategy:

  • Develop 5-8 signature cocktails that are YOUR brand
  • Include 2-3 craft mocktail options (15-20% of guests don't drink)
  • Offer seasonal specials (pumpkin spice mule in fall, watermelon cooler in summer)
  • Batch your signatures for speed -- pre-batched cocktails pour in 10 seconds vs. 90 seconds to build
Homework: Partner creates 3 signature cocktails to taste-test at the next meeting. Name them something fun.

The Timeline -- What Happens Next?
15 minutes
+

End the meeting with clear next steps and deadlines. Nothing kills momentum like leaving without a plan.

Suggested launch timeline:

  • Week 1-2: Form LLC, open business bank account, get ServSafe certified
  • Week 2-4: Call ABC board, get insurance quotes, start trailer shopping
  • Month 2-3: Acquire and convert trailer (or start with portable setup)
  • Month 3-4: Build website, set up social media, do a styled shoot for photos
  • Month 4-5: List on The Knot/WeddingWire, connect with local venues and planners
  • Month 5-6: Book first events! Start with friends/family events at a discount to build reviews
Set a target launch date. Having a deadline makes everything real.
Set the next meeting date (1 week from now)
Assign ownership on action items from this meeting
Create a shared Google Doc to track progress
