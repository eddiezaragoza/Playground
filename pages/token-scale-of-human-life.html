🧠 The Core Challenge

Tokens are text. Life is... not text. So how do we bridge the gap?

A token is roughly 4 characters or 0.75 words in English. It's the fundamental unit of how AI processes information -- reading input, generating output, "thinking" through problems.

Mapping tokens to human activities means asking a deceptively deep question: How much information does a human brain actually process during any given activity?

Here's the wild part: Your brain processes about 11 million bits of sensory input per second [1], but your conscious mind only handles about 10 bits per second [2]. That's a 1,000,000x gap between what you're absorbing and what you're aware of.

So the question becomes: are we counting the autopilot stuff (your hands gripping the steering wheel, your lungs breathing) or just the conscious decisions -- the stuff you'd need to write down if you were teaching a robot to do it?

We're going with a blend: conscious decision-making plus the information you'd need to describe or replicate the activity from scratch. Think of it as the "instruction manual" token count.

🔢 The Math Checks Out

Let's validate that 40,000-token driving estimate.

Take a 10-minute drive. You make a conscious micro-decision roughly every half-second: check mirror, adjust speed, read a sign, track the car ahead, scan an intersection. Research suggests drivers make approximately 20 decisions per mile [4] -- at 30 mph over 10 minutes, that's 5 miles and roughly 100 major decisions, with hundreds more micro-adjustments in between.

Why Some Activities Are "Cheap"

Autopilot activities cost almost nothing. Brushing teeth clocks in at ~1,000 tokens because you're barely thinking. Your motor cortex handles it while your conscious mind wanders. But driving? Even though it feels automatic, your brain is burning through context like a GPU running inference -- constantly evaluating, predicting, and adjusting.

Why Creative Work Is the Most Expensive

Writing, designing, coding -- anything where you're generating novel output -- burns tokens fast. That's literally what AI does too, which is why a complex session can hit 500K+ tokens. A human doing the same work runs a similar "token count" internally. It just takes hours instead of seconds.

🤯 The Mind-Bender

Reframing your day through the lens of tokens.

Your average day is probably 1-2 million tokens of conscious processing. A heavy AI session that builds something complex might hit 300K-500K tokens. In raw "cognitive work" terms, that's compressing a quarter to half of your workday's brainpower into 30 minutes.

Or put another way -- every time a 2-minute AI task replaces 3 hours of human work, that's roughly 200,000 tokens of human cognitive effort saved.

The real flex: AI processes tokens about 1,000x faster than human conscious thought [3]. But humans have that 11-million-bit-per-second sensory engine running underneath that AI can't touch [1]. You can catch a ball thrown at your face. AI can't. That's an 11-million-token-per-second flex.

At the end of the day, tokens are just a unit of measurement -- like calories or steps. But framing your daily life through them reveals something genuinely interesting: the most mundane parts of being human are computationally wild. Your morning commute is a supercomputer-grade operation. Your brain just makes it feel boring.
